2018 is turning out to be California's most destructive and expensive wildfire season on record, with $432 million dollars already spent on firefighting and containment. Cal Fire director Ken Pimlott asked lawmakers for another $234 million in early September, the earliest the agency has ever requested emergency funds, to prepare for the peak of the fire season that traditionally runs through the fall.

A report released Aug. 27, the fourth in a series of climate change assessments comissioned by the state, found that if global warming continues at the rate, California residents can expect more deadlier weather patterns, including longer droughts, higher temperatures, and bigger wildfires. But how did the most populous state get to this point?

  • California is a heavily forested state

    California covers a total of 100 million acres, and about a third of it is covered by forests and grassland. The state contains more forest than any other in the country except Alaska.

    Source: University of California Forest Research and Outreach

  • California is increasingly hot and dry

    Hot and dry temperatures due to global warming is taking its toll on West Coast forests. The past five years in California have been the hottest on record, and the state recently came out of a TK long drought, its second worst in history.

    A lack of rainfall coupled with disappearing groundwater increases the liklihood of tree death. Dead trees serve as explosive fuel when wildfires start.

    Source: California Fourth Climate Assessment

  • There are millions of dead trees in California

    Because of the drought, rising temperatures, and a growing epidemic of migrating bark beetles that prey on trees, an alarming number of trees, nearly 129 million, have died since 2010. California has removed only 1.3 million of these trees in that same period; the rest litter the forests with tinder.

    Source: Cal Fire Tree Mortality Map

  • Much of California is a time bomb

    The large number of dead trees, combined with California's already dry, hot, and windy climate, has made much of California susceptible to wildfires. It doesn't take much to start one - humans are responsible for XX% of them - and with about 1/4 of counties facing severe or worsening drought conditions, rain and groundwater is often unavailable to help put out fires.

    Source: U.S. Drought Monitor

  • People are moving into high fire-risk zones

    California's population grew by 3 million between 2000 and 2010, and according to the risk management company Verisk, in 2017 over a quarter of California's population lived near moderate or high-risk fire corridors.

    With this increase in population comes a higher possibility of a human-made wildfire. In addition, as people move into these area, more buildings are in harm's way, and structures generally burn longer than vegetation, allowing fire more time to spread.

    Source: Verisk Wildfire Risk Report

  • What's being done?

    Governor Jerry Brown and other California Democratic leaders are attempting to impose regulations to combat climate change. The state recently passed a bill that doubles down on sustainability and renewable energy targets across the board; 50% by 2026, 60% by 2030, and 100% by 2045. With this, and a commitment to perform more prescribed burns to reduce the amount of flammable material on public lands, the state hopes to rein in an out-of-control fire season.

    Source: California Fourth Climate Assessment
