  • The deadline

    A position on the Supreme Court becomes available when one of the nine sitting justices resigns, retires or dies. Once a position becomes vacant, the president nominates someone whom the Senate will then either confirm or reject. It took four months for Neil Gorsuch, Trump’s first pick for the supreme court, to be confirmed. In order for Trump to lockdown a third pick, a spot would have open up by July 2020 before the election in November of that year.

    So whose spot is most likely to be vacated?

  • Breyer crosses the average retirement age

    Justice Anthony Kennedy, whose recently announced retirement created the spot for XXXXX, retired at 81 - an age Breyer will hit before 2020. Kennedy's retirement was far from an outlier with 80 being the average retirement age for the last 11 justices. There's a slim chance that Breyer might want to do the same. However, given his history of voting with the liberal end of the bench, he'll more than likely try to hold on and hand his position off to a Democratic president.

  • Ginsburg retirement plans

    Ruth Bader Ginsburg, at 85, is already the fourth oldest supreme court justice of all-time. However, recent comments suggest she won’t be stopping there, citing John Paul Stevens, who retired at a spritely 90-years-old in 2010, as her model.

    Plus, despite her age, she was nominated quite late and has only been in the job for 24 years. That’s three years less than the average.

  • How long can Ginsburg go?

    It’s tough to hide from the fact that she’s the oldest current justice and is already five years over the average retirement age for the court. However, anyone who saw her workout plan in the recent documentary RBG would struggle to argue that she isn’t fighting fit.

    Ginsburg has also recently brought four new law clerks onboard until 2020 and has been on the record of saying “As long as I can do the job full steam, I will be here.”.

  • Justice Thomas is now the longest serving

    Breyer and Ginsburg are both Democrat nominated judges but there’s a small chance that we could see a retirement on the Republican nominated side. Clarence Thomas, while relatively young at 70, has already served for 26 years, just one less than the average and four less than recent retiree Kennedy.

    So it’s entirely reasonable that Thomas believes he’s served his time. If he decided this on Trump’s watch, the Republicans, while they wouldn’t gain a seat on the conservative side, would be able to lock another seat down with a younger judge.

  • The caveat (or hope)

    However, the entire scenario changes if the Democrats manage to flip the Senate at the midterms in November. If a seat was then to become vacant, the Democrats could choose to block any nomination that Trump makes. And they would likely feel justified by the recent precedent - after the Republicans refused to confirm Obama’s third pick in Merrick Garland in 2016.
